History of the Duquesne Incline

A Pittsburgh landmark since 1877

Our Story

The Early Years

The Duquesne Incline was built by Samuel Diescher and completed in 1877. It was one of many inclines constructed during Pittsburgh's industrial boom in the late 19th century, designed primarily to transport residents of the growing communities on Mount Washington, then called Coal Hill, to jobs along the riverfront.

Unlike most of Pittsburgh's inclines which have since been dismantled, the Duquesne Incline has survived for nearly 150 years, becoming an enduring symbol of the city's industrial heritage.

Decline and Revival

By the mid-20th century, with the decline of Pittsburgh's steel industry and changes in transportation, many of the city's inclines were closed. The Duquesne Incline nearly suffered the same fate when it was closed in 1962 due to declining ridership and increased maintenance costs.

However, in a remarkable display of community spirit, local residents formed the Society for the Preservation of the Duquesne Heights Incline. They raised funds to purchase and preserve the incline, reopening it in 1963 as both a working transportation system and a historic landmark.

The Duquesne Incline, circa 1900

Engineering Marvel

The Duquesne Incline operates using a counterbalance system, with two cars permanently attached to opposite ends of the same cable. As one car ascends, the other descends, passing each other at the midpoint.

Technical Details

  • Length: 800 feet
  • Grade: 30.5 degrees
  • Elevation Change: 400 feet
  • Original Power: Steam engine
  • Current Power: Electric motor
  • Original Opening: May 20, 1877

The original wooden cars were replaced with the current steel-framed cars in 1887, but the original hoisting machinery, installed in 1877, is still in operation today, making it one of the oldest pieces of continuously operating mechanical systems in the nation.

The Incline Museum

When you visit the Duquesne Incline, don't miss our museum in the upper station. The museum features:

  • A collection of historic photographs documenting Pittsburgh's development
  • Models and displays explaining the mechanical operation of the incline
  • Artifacts from Pittsburgh's industrial past
  • Information about the other inclines that once served Mount Washington

The museum also features a viewing platform offering spectacular panoramic views of downtown Pittsburgh and its three rivers.

Timeline

1877

Grand Opening

The Duquesne Incline opened on May 20, 1877, designed by Samuel Diescher and built at a cost of $47,000.

1887

Car Replacement

The original wooden cars were replaced with steel-framed cars, which are still in use today.

1932

Conversion to Electricity

The incline was converted from steam power to electric motors.

1962

Threatened Closure

The incline was closed due to declining ridership and increasing maintenance costs.

1963

Community Rescue

The Society for the Preservation of the Duquesne Heights Incline was formed by local residents who raised funds to reopen the incline.

1974

National Historic Designation

The Duquesne Incline was listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

2023

Ongoing Preservation

Continuous restoration efforts ensure the incline remains operational for future generations to enjoy.
